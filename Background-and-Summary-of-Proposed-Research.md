# Background and Summary of Proposed Research (SurveilAMR)

Antimicrobial resistance (AMR) is among the greatest threats to modern medicine: infections that were once easily treatable now fail more often, prolong illness, raise costs, and increase mortality. Across health systems especially in low- and middle-income settings—resistance data is increasingly collected, yet it remains fragmented, analyzed slowly, and rarely translated into guidance that clinicians and public health teams can use at the moment decisions are made.

SurveilAMR addresses this gap by applying artificial intelligence to structured surveillance data from the Vivli AMR Register. The project will integrate organism, susceptibility, and contextual metadata to detect resistance trends, compare patterns across sites and regions, and generate timely, interpretable dashboards and alerts for clinical and population-level decision-making—turning a growing global data asset into practical intelligence that researchers and frontline teams can act on.

**Improving patient outcomes.** By surfacing local and regional resistance profiles earlier, SurveilAMR supports more appropriate empiric antibiotic choices—particularly in settings where culture results are delayed—reducing treatment failure, complications, and length of stay when therapy reflects real-world susceptibility patterns.

**Strengthening stewardship.** Decision-support views will help clinicians and antimicrobial stewardship programs identify high-risk resistance phenotypes, prioritize targeted therapy, and discourage unnecessary broad-spectrum use—preserving the effectiveness of remaining antibiotics.

**Informing public health practice.** Automated trend detection, geographic mapping, and early-warning signals will help authorities update treatment guidelines, target infection prevention, and respond sooner to emerging resistance threats.

**Strengthening health systems.** SurveilAMR embeds usable analytics into routine surveillance workflows, improving data interpretation, reporting efficiency, and the capacity of health systems to monitor and respond to AMR at scale.

Together, these aims advance the 2026 Vivli AMR Surveillance Data Challenge: expanding use of the AMR Register, supporting new analytical tools, and ensuring surveillance data drives better care, smarter prescribing, and stronger public health action.
